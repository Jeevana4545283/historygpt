Eugen Goldstein (5 September 1850 – 25 December 1930) was a German physicist. He was an early investigator of discharge tubes, the discoverer of anode rays (also called canal rays), and is sometimes credited with the discovery of the proton. In 1876, Goldstein showed that cathode rays could cast shadows and were emitted perpendicular to the cathode surface. This discovery led to the term "cathode rays". In 1886, he discovered canal rays (Kanalstrahlen)—also known as anode rays or positive rays. He found that Crookes tubes with a perforated cathode emit a glow at the end of the tube opposite the anode. Goldstein concluded that, in addition to the cathode rays traveling from the cathode to the anode, there are other rays traveling in the opposite direction. Since these rays passed through the holes or channels in the cathode, he called them "canal rays". They consisted of positive ions whose identity depended on the gas inside the tube. His work laid the foundations for the discovery of the proton by Rutherford and the field of mass spectrometry.
